ADHD

ADHD is Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. The name is not particularly accurate. ADHD is not simply a deficit of attention. It is a difference in how the brain manages attention, motivation, impulse control, and emotional regulation, driven largely by how the dopamine system works.

It is a lifelong neurological profile. It is not caused by bad parenting, too much screen time, or lack of effort.

ADHD comes in three presentations: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, and combined.

How the ADHD brain actually works

The key is dopamine. Dopamine is the brain's motivation and reward system. In ADHD, this system works differently. The brain does not produce or use dopamine in the same way, which means the automatic processes that most people take for granted require conscious effort.

The result is a brain that struggles to engage with tasks it finds unstimulating, cannot easily filter attention, shifts rapidly between focus and disengagement, and reacts more strongly to frustration.

The same brain, given something genuinely interesting, can produce hyperfocus. An intense, sustained, highly productive engagement.

What it looks like at school

ADHD in a school setting runs into specific friction points:
- Sitting still for extended periods goes against how the ADHD nervous system regulates itself.
- Starting tasks is genuinely hard. This looks like avoidance but is often difficulty with initiation.
- Transitions are disruptive.
- Time blindness is real. Deadlines do not produce the same urgency.
- Emotional regulation is harder.
- The gap between ability and output is often significant.

What it looks like at home

The ADHD child at home after a school day has usually spent hours suppressing impulses and forcing attention. By the time they get home the regulatory system is depleted.

This shows as inability to do homework, explosive reactions, inability to transition off screens, and chaotic behaviour before exhaustion.

Screen and gaming use is a specific issue. The ADHD brain responds strongly to the unpredictable reward loops in games. This makes disengagement genuinely hard.

What to ask for

Reasonable adjustments for ADHD:
- Movement breaks built into the school day.
- Flexible seating (wobble cushions, standing).
- Tasks broken into shorter segments with clear checkpoints.
- Timers and visual time cues.
- Proximity to the teacher, away from distractions.
- Extended time in assessments.
- Reduced homework load.
- A trusted adult to check in with.

How to describe ADHD clearly in meetings

Avoid "he just cannot concentrate". Be specific.

"My son has significant difficulty initiating tasks without external structure and support. He can hyperfocus for extended periods on topics he finds genuinely engaging, but requires scaffolding to begin work in most school contexts. He experiences emotional dysregulation under frustration..."

On diagnosis and waiting lists

NHS waiting times for ADHD assessment in England are significant, often two to four years.

You do not need a diagnosis to request support, ask for reasonable adjustments, or pursue an EHCP. What helps while you wait is consistent, dated documentation.
