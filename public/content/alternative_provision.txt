Alternative Provision: What It Is and What Your Rights Are

Alternative Provision, AP, is the part of the education system that sits outside mainstream and special schools. It includes Pupil Referral Units, AP academies and free schools, hospital schools, and a large and largely unregulated sector of private and voluntary providers.

Around 43,000 children are in some form of AP at any given time in England. The majority have SEND. Many have EHC plans. Most arrived there through a route that involved unmet need, exclusion, or both.

What Alternative Provision is

AP is education arranged:
- by local authorities for pupils who, because of exclusion, illness or other reasons, would not otherwise receive suitable education
- by schools for pupils who are suspended
- by schools for pupils who are directed to off-site provision to improve behaviour

Types of AP:
- Pupil Referral Units (PRUs): LA-maintained schools.
- AP academies and free schools: Independently run but state-funded.
- School-arranged AP: Off-site provision commissioned by a school (often private/voluntary).
- LA-funded placements: Non-state provision.

Who is in Alternative Provision

- 16,600 pupils in state-funded AP schools (Jan 2025)
- 26,400 in school-arranged AP (Jan 2024)
- 80% of LA AP pupils have an EHC plan or SEN statement
- 55% eligible for free school meals (vs 25% nationally)

Children in AP are disproportionately eligible for free school meals, from specific ethnic minority backgrounds, excluded children, and children with unmet SEND.

The outcomes picture

Outcomes for pupils in AP are significantly worse than for pupils in mainstream and special schools. Pass rates for five or more GCSEs at grade C/4 and above are in the low single figures in percentage terms.

Your rights if your child is placed in Alternative Provision

If your child has an EHC plan:
The EHC plan continues to apply. The provision specified in the plan must be delivered. You retain all rights including annual review and appeal. The AP setting must make provision consistent with the plan.

Without an EHC plan but with identified SEND:
The Equality Act 2010 still applies. The AP setting must make reasonable adjustments. Placement in AP is evidence of significant need and may be grounds to request an EHC needs assessment.

The quality and registration question

Not all AP settings are registered as schools. If your child is placed in an unregistered setting, ask:
- Is this setting registered?
- Has it been inspected?
- What is the curriculum offer?
- How long is the placement intended to last?
- What is the plan for return?

Directed off-site provision by schools

Schools can direct pupils to off-site provision without formally excluding them. This is legal in specific, limited circumstances.

The school must:
- be satisfied the provision is suitable
- ensure health and safety
- ensure quality
- keep the pupil on the school roll
- ensure attendance is monitored

A school cannot use off-site direction as a substitute for exclusion or to manage a child with challenging needs without proper process.
