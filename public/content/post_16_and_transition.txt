Young People Aged 16 to 25

The SEND system does not end at 16. It does change. And the changes catch a lot of families off guard.

This page explains what the law says about EHCP rights beyond school age, what happens at each key transition point, and what to watch out for.

What the law says

An EHCP can remain in force until the end of the academic year in which a young person turns 25.

This is not automatic. The plan continues as long as the young person is in education or training and still needs the provision specified in it. The law is clear: a local authority cannot cease an EHCP simply because a young person has reached 19. It must assess whether the outcomes in the plan have been achieved.

The legal basis is Section 46 of the Children and Families Act 2014.

EHCPs do not extend to higher education (university).

Key transition points

At 16:
Decision-making rights shift from parent to young person. The young person becomes the primary decision-maker in EHCP processes, including appeals. Parents can still support.

At 18:
Parental responsibility ends. Adult social care becomes relevant (Care Act assessment should be requested).

Year 9 (age 13-14):
Transition planning must start at the year 9 annual review. Every review from then on must focus on preparing for adulthood (employment, independent living, community, health).

Moving to further education:
If moving to an FE college, the LA must issue an amended final plan naming the college by 31 March of the year of transfer.

If the LA tries to end the EHCP at 16 or 19

This is happening more frequently. An LA cannot cease an EHCP simply because a young person has turned 16, 18, or 19.

It can only cease if:
- the LA is no longer responsible for the young person, or
- it determines the plan is no longer necessary (outcomes have been achieved).

If the LA sends a cease to maintain notice, you have the right to appeal to the SEND Tribunal. The plan continues while the appeal is live.

Young people who do not yet have an EHCP

A young person can request an EHC needs assessment for the first time up to their 25th birthday. They do not have to have had one at school.

The Care Act and adult social care

When your young person turns 18, responsibility for social care provision moves to adult services under the Care Act 2014. Request a Care Act assessment before they turn 18. This is not automatic.

Access to Work

Access to Work is a government grant scheme that pays for practical support in the workplace for people with disabilities or health conditions. It applies to paid jobs, apprenticeships, and some work experience. It is underused by young people with SEND.
