Exclusions and Your Child's Rights

Children with SEND are excluded from school at significantly higher rates than their peers. That is not a coincidence. It is a failure of the system to provide the support those children need.

This page explains what exclusions are, what your rights are, and what you can do if your child is excluded or is at risk of being excluded.

The numbers

- 5x more likely to be permanently excluded if identified SEN
- 11.09 suspensions per 100 pupils on SEN Support
- 2.46 suspensions per 100 pupils without SEN
- 10.31 suspensions per 100 pupils with EHC plans

The most common stated reason for exclusion is persistent disruptive behaviour. In many cases involving SEND children, that behaviour is a direct consequence of unmet need.

What an exclusion is

There are two types.
- Suspension (fixed-term exclusion): the child is sent home for a set number of days. Max 45 days in a year.
- Permanent exclusion: the child is removed from the school roll entirely.

There is also a category that does not appear in official statistics: illegal exclusions (off-rolling or informal exclusions). These include:
- being sent home informally without a formal exclusion being issued
- being told not to come in on certain days
- being placed in isolation repeatedly without educational justification
- being put on a reduced timetable without agreement
- being encouraged to leave or move to a different setting

None of these are lawful as substitutes for a formal exclusion process.

What the law says

If your child has an EHC plan, the school must provide the support specified in that plan. Failure to do so, which then results in behaviour that leads to exclusion, is a failure of the school's legal duty.

If your child does not have an EHC plan but has identified SEN, the school must still make reasonable adjustments under the Equality Act 2010.

If your child is suspended

The school must tell you in writing on the day of the suspension. The letter must state the reason, length, and your rights to make representations.

Your child must continue to receive full-time education from the sixth school day of any exclusion. The school is responsible for arranging this.

If your child is permanently excluded

The headteacher must notify you in writing on the day. You have the right to make representations to the governing body.

If the governing body upholds the exclusion, you have the right to request an Independent Review Panel (IRP). If your child has SEND, you can request that a SEND expert sits on the IRP. This is your right.

The informal exclusion problem

Informal exclusions include being sent home early "to avoid an incident", being asked not to come in, or being put on a reduced timetable without written agreement.

A reduced timetable can only be in place legally if it has your written agreement and is subject to regular review. It cannot be indefinite.

What to do right now

If your child has been excluded or is at risk of exclusion:
1. Get the paperwork. Ask for the formal exclusion letter.
2. Note everything. Date, time, what was said.
3. Contact IPSEA or SOS!SEN for free legal advice.
4. Contact your local SENDIASS service.
5. Request an emergency annual review if your child has an EHCP.
