Autism

Autism is a lifelong neurological difference. It affects how a person processes sensory information, communicates, builds social understanding, and experiences the world around them. It is not a mental health condition. It is not caused by parenting. It is not something to grow out of.

What autism actually is

The core of autism is a different way of processing.

Sensory input arrives differently. Social information is processed differently. Patterns and routines carry more weight. Unexpected change costs more. Communication, particularly the unspoken social kind, requires more conscious effort.

None of this is visible from the outside. Which is part of the problem.

An autistic child in a mainstream classroom is often doing an enormous amount of invisible work just to stay in the room. Managing sensory input that feels amplified. Decoding social cues. Following routines. Holding together a presentation of coping that does not reflect what is actually happening internally.

What it looks like at school

Autism presents differently across children. There is no single profile.

Some autistic children are quiet, compliant, and apparently fine until they are not. This is particularly common in girls, and in children who have learned to mask their differences effectively.

Some autistic children are visibly distressed in school. By noise, by change, by social demands, by sensory overload.

Some autistic children are twice exceptional. Highly able academically while having significant support needs in other areas.

Consistent themes in school:
- Transitions are hard. The space between lessons, between activities.
- Unstructured time is often harder than structured time. Break and lunch can be the most difficult parts of the day.
- Explicit is easier than implicit. Direct communication works better than hints.
- Sensory environment matters. Lighting, noise, texture, smell.

What it looks like at home

The child who coped at school often does not cope at home. This is not a contradiction. It is the cost of masking.

Meltdowns after school, refusal to eat, inability to transition, extreme reactions to small changes. These are common and explainable. Home is where the mask comes off.

What to ask for

Reasonable adjustments for autistic children do not require a diagnosis. They require a clear description of need.

Common and effective adjustments:
- Advance notice of changes to routine.
- A quiet space available when overload builds.
- Structured support during unstructured time.
- Explicit rather than implicit communication.
- Written or visual timetables.
- Sensory adjustments (ear defenders, clothing).
- A named adult to go to.

How to describe autism clearly in meetings

Avoid "she finds things difficult". Be specific.

"My daughter experiences significant sensory overload in noisy environments... When overload builds she becomes unable to process verbal instruction... By the time she reaches home she has typically been in a suppressed state for several hours and frequently has meltdowns lasting between 20 minutes and an hour."

That is specific. It points to a clear need.

On diagnosis and waiting lists

Waiting times for autism assessment through the NHS currently run to years in most parts of England.

You do not have to wait. A school can identify a child as having SEND and put support in place based on observed need, without a diagnosis. An EHCP assessment can be requested without a diagnosis.

What helps while you wait is evidence. Document what you observe.
