## Quick Read Summary

### SEND today: a quick read for parents

Everything across this site, brought together in one page. Where things stand, what is confirmed, what is not, and what the next 6 to 12 months are likely to look like.

**Why this page exists:** The SEND Navigator covers a lot of ground across eight detailed pages. This page brings all of that together into **one short, grounded summary** you can read in a few minutes.

#### Where things stand right now

The SEND system in England is under severe strain. **Around 576,000 children have an Education, Health and Care Plan**, a 140% increase since 2015. Nearly 1.9 million young people are identified with SEND overall.

The law is clear. The problem is delivery. **Only around 60% of EHCPs are issued within the 20-week legal timescale.** Many families wait six to twelve months. Nearly 50% of parents describe securing support as stressful. 22% say their child's school is not delivering what is written in the plan.

Councils are in financial crisis. **95% are running SEND budget deficits.** Spending on high needs has risen from £7.8 billion to £13.1 billion in under a decade. The system is widely described as unsustainable.

#### What is actually changing

Some changes are no longer speculative. They have been publicly confirmed and funded.

- The government will **write off 90% of council SEND deficits**, a rescue package worth over £5 billion. From 2028 to 2029, central government takes full responsibility for SEND spending growth.
- **£3.7 billion is creating 60,000 new SEND places**, including new specialist schools and specialist units in mainstream.
- Every secondary school is expected to have an **inclusion base**.
- **£200 million is going into SEND teacher training.** Ofsted now has an "Inclusion" judgement in school inspections.
- National SEND standards are being developed, with the government saying **it will legislate to give them legal force**.

**None of these confirmed changes remove existing legal rights.**

#### What has not changed

**The Children and Families Act 2014 is still the law.** EHCPs are still legally enforceable. The 20-week timescale still applies. Parents still have the right to request assessment, choose a school, and appeal decisions.

Tribunal outcomes still heavily favour parents, with **around 90% of decided cases won**. No child has lost their EHCP because of reform discussions. No existing plan has been removed or downgraded.

#### What is being discussed

Alongside confirmed changes, there is active public debate about deeper reform. **Nothing in this area is law. Nothing has been enacted.**

- A **four-tier model of support** has been discussed, with EHCPs reserved for the most severe and complex needs.
- There is debate about **mandatory mediation before tribunal** and shifting funding to school clusters rather than individual plans.
- The National Education Union has urged the government to maintain legal thresholds. The **Save Our Children's Rights campaign gathered over 130,000 signatures**.

The tension sits between system efficiency and individual safeguards. **These debates are shaping policy, but they are not policy yet.**

#### What we do not know yet

Key questions remain unanswered. **No draft legislation has been published** setting out changes to EHCP eligibility. No final national standards have been published. No confirmed funding model replacing EHCP-linked funding has been agreed.

Parents want to know whether existing EHCPs will be grandfathered, what happens to post-16 support, how neurodivergent conditions will be treated, and whether lower-tier support will be legally enforceable. **These questions do not have answers yet.**

#### What the leaks are and are not

There has been a steady flow of unconfirmed reports from outlets including The Guardian, iNews, and the Financial Times. They describe proposals like a four-tier system, digital passports for SEND, and possible implementation delays to 2029.

**None of these are government policy. None have legal force.** Leaks are how ideas get tested. Strong public reaction, including the 130,000-signature petition and Labour MP pushback, has already slowed timelines and increased emphasis on co-production.

**A leak does not mean a decision has been made. A headline does not override legislation.**

#### What happens next

**The next six to twelve months look like this:**

- **Early 2026:** a SEND-focused White Paper is expected, setting out formal proposals for the first time.
- **Autumn 2026:** councils must submit local SEND reform plans to qualify for the deficit bailout.
- **By 2027:** all local areas inspected under the new Ofsted and CQC SEND framework.
- **2028 to 2029:** central government takes full responsibility for SEND spending growth.
- **2029 to 2030 (unconfirmed):** leaked reports suggest full implementation of major reforms might not happen until then.

Any change to rights requires consultation, legislation, and parliamentary scrutiny. **None of that has started yet.**

#### What this means for you right now

**Your child's rights today are real. They are enforceable. They do not disappear because of a headline.**

- If your child needs an EHCP, the legal route to get one has not changed.
- If you are in the middle of an assessment, keep going. The same law applies.
- If provision is not being delivered, you still have routes to challenge.
- Stay informed, not consumed. Engage with consultations when they open.

Reform is coming because the current system is failing too many families. That does not mean the solution will automatically be better. It does mean **there is space for challenge, shaping, and accountability**.

**You are not behind. You are not too late. Your child's rights do not expire while policy is being discussed.**

## Where We Are Now

### Current situation: where is SEND provision in the UK today

**Status: Confirmed** | Last updated: 15th February 2026

#### 60-Second Summary

In the UK, SEND support is not one single system. Each nation runs its own framework. **This page focuses on England** because the legal duties, the plan process, and the rights attached to an Education, Health and Care Plan are specific to England.

In England, SEND support mainly sits in two routes. SEN Support is the help a nursery, school or college provides without a legal plan. **Education, Health and Care Plans are legal plans that set out a child or young person's needs, outcomes and the provision that must be put in place.**

The core problem is not that the law is unclear. The law exists. The problem is that **the system is overloaded, inconsistent by area, and too often slow and adversarial**. Families end up having to push hard for what should be straightforward early help.

#### How will this impact children and parents

If you are a parent or carer, the lived reality can feel like this.

You may be told support is available, but **it is not consistent or timely**. You may be asked to wait, repeat your story, or prove the impact again and again. You may feel you have to become the project manager, advocate, and legal researcher, while also parenting.

For children, delays matter. **Support that arrives late can mean missed learning, rising anxiety, school refusal, exclusions, or behaviour that gets labelled as the problem instead of the signal.** For families, the pressure is cumulative. It affects work, finances, mental health, and trust in services.

None of this means good support is impossible. Many settings and professionals work extremely hard. **The issue is the system design, the capacity, and the variation.**

#### Statistics and facts

- **1.9m** young people with SEND (0–25 age group)
- **576,000** children with an EHCP (140% rise since 2015)
- **1 in 5** KS4 pupils with SEND (nearly 20% and rising)
- **£13.1bn** council SEND spending (up from £7.8bn in 2015)
- **95%** of councils in SEND deficit (79% risked insolvency)
- **60%** EHCPs issued on time (within 20-week limit)
- **90%** parents win at tribunal (of decided cases)
- **50%** parents find it stressful (securing SEND support)
- Independent specialist school costs have risen **206% since 2015 to £1.7 billion a year**.
- The National Audit Office calls the system "not delivering value for money".
- The IFS warns SEND cost pressures are **"crowding out" mainstream education funds**.

#### The structure of support in England

In England, SEND support has two main layers.

SEN Support is the day to day support a setting provides without an EHCP. It might include adjustments in the classroom, targeted interventions, small group work, or additional adult support. In theory, this is where most needs should be met early.

**Education, Health and Care Plans are different. They are legal documents.** They set out needs, provision, and outcomes, and they name a placement. The local authority is responsible for making sure the provision in the plan is delivered. This legal enforceability is why EHCPs matter so much to families.

#### Why the system feels like a fight

There is a consistent pattern.

The EHCP route is slow. It is also paperwork heavy. Even though there is a 20 week legal deadline, **only around half of new plans are completed on time**. Many families wait far longer.

When families are refused, delayed, or offered too little, many escalate. The tribunal numbers and parent success rates show this is not rare. **When parents win 95% of cases that reach judgement, it suggests that a lot of children were initially refused support they were entitled to.**

This creates a loop:
- Families push harder because they feel they have to.
- Local authorities feel overwhelmed and financially trapped.
- Schools feel caught in the middle, trying to cope while waiting for decisions and funding.
- **Trust falls, conflict rises, and children wait.**

#### The inconsistency problem

Postcode variation is one of the biggest recurring problems. **Thresholds and practice can differ across areas**, which means what works in one place can fail completely in another. That uncertainty drives anxiety and escalation, because families cannot rely on a clear baseline offer.

#### Why EHCP demand keeps rising

There is **an over-reliance on EHCPs for support that should sit earlier**. When SEN Support is not strong enough, an EHCP starts to feel like the only reliable mechanism to secure provision and accountability. That drives demand upwards, which then increases system load and cost, which then drives more rationing behaviour.

#### The financial pressure underneath everything

The financial picture is severe. Council spending on high needs has risen from £7.8 billion to £13.1 billion in under a decade. **95% of councils are running SEND deficits**, and by March 2026, 43% are forecast to have deficits so large they equal or exceed their available reserves.

Without intervention, **79% of councils risked insolvency by 2028** due to SEND debts alone. That financial stress is not separate from the family experience. It shapes behaviour, delays, gatekeeping, and disputes.

There are also perverse incentives baked in. Schools do not get extra funding per child unless there is an EHCP, so there is a financial disincentive to take high needs pupils without one. **Councils are financially liable for EHCPs but not for early intervention**, so money gets spent reactively on crisis placements and legal battles rather than early support.

#### A note on the rest of the UK

**Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland have different systems and legal routes.** This section covers England only.

## What Is Changing

### Confirmed changes: what changes are coming to SEND provision in 2026 and have been confirmed

**Status: Confirmed** | Last updated: 15th February 2026

#### 60-Second Summary

Some changes to SEND in England are no longer speculative. **They have been publicly set out, funded, or formally announced**, even if not fully delivered yet.

**These confirmed changes do not remove current legal rights.** Education, Health and Care Plans still exist. Tribunal rights still exist. The Children and Families Act 2014 is still in force.

What is changing is how the system is being prepared to work differently. The focus is on **clearer national expectations, earlier support, less bureaucracy, better workforce capacity, and reducing the extreme pressure points** that currently force families into crisis routes.

Most of these changes are being **phased in through guidance, programmes, pilots, and capital investment**, with legislation expected later. As of February 2026, **local systems are already being instructed to prepare for reform**, making this a transition phase rather than a waiting phase.

#### How will this impact children and parents

In the short term, **most families will not see an overnight difference**. Rights and processes remain the same today.

Over time, if these changes work as intended, families should experience **clearer expectations, fewer delays, and less need to fight for basic support**. Schools should be better equipped to respond earlier. Local authorities should be more consistent and more accountable.

However, **impact will depend on delivery**. Training takes time. Capacity takes time. Digital systems take time. Until those changes bed in, families may still experience the same pressures they face now.

#### Statistics and facts

- **£5bn+** council debt bailout (90% of SEND deficits written off)
- **£3.7bn** capital investment (60,000 new SEND places)
- **£200m** teacher training (SEND specialist programme)
- **60,000** new SEND places (30–40% increase nationally)
- **£300m** Safety Valve agreements (most indebted councils)
- **400+** new educational psychologists (training pipeline)
- From **2028 to 2029, central government will cover SEND spending in full**.
- Every secondary school is expected to have an **"inclusion base"**.
- Ofsted has introduced a new **"Inclusion" judgement in school inspections**.
- National SEND standards are confirmed with **consultation planned for 2026 to 2027**.
- Nine Regional Expert Partnerships are testing new approaches, and a **digital EHCP template is being piloted**.
- **Safety Valve agreements (£300 million)** and Delivering Better Value in SEND (£85 million for 55 councils) continue.
- All local areas will be inspected under the new **Ofsted and CQC area SEND framework by 2027**.
- A **joint DfE and NHS England letter** has instructed local authorities and Integrated Care Boards to begin preparing for SEND reform now.

#### National SEND standards

The government has confirmed that **national SEND standards will be introduced**. These standards are intended to set out what support should ordinarily be available for children and young people with SEND, regardless of where they live.

The purpose is to **reduce postcode variation** and to make it clearer what should happen at SEN Support level, before families are pushed towards an EHCP.

#### Digital first and standardised EHCP processes

The move towards digital EHCP processes is confirmed. The aim is to **reduce paperwork, improve transparency, and make it easier for families to see what is happening and when**. **This is not a new legal process. It is a different way of running the same legal process.**

#### Workforce training and capacity building

The government has confirmed **significant investment in SEND workforce development**: mandatory SEND training for teachers, expanded SENCO training, increased training routes for educational psychologists, and development of specialist SEND qualifications.

#### Expansion of specialist provision

**New specialist schools are being built and funded.** This is a confirmed capital programme. **This does not remove the presumption of mainstream education**, but it acknowledges that some children need specialist environments.

#### Local inclusion planning and accountability

Local areas are being required to **plan SEND provision across education, health and care more jointly**. Where areas repeatedly fail, the Department for Education now has **clearer intervention powers**.

#### Funding direction and deficit absorption

In February 2026, the government confirmed it will **write off 90% of council SEND deficits**, a rescue package worth over £5 billion. From 2028 to 2029, **central government will take full responsibility for SEND spending growth**. This is a fundamental shift.

#### What has not changed yet

**None of these confirmed changes remove existing rights.** EHCPs remain legally enforceable. Appeal routes remain. Timescales remain. The confirmed changes are about **preparation, capacity, and consistency, not about removing protections**.

## What Has Not Changed

### What has not changed: what remains the same in the SEND system as of 15 February 2026

**Status: Confirmed** | Last updated: 15th February 2026

#### 60-Second Summary

Despite the volume of discussion about reform, **the core legal structure of SEND in England has not changed**.

Education, Health and Care Plans still exist. **The Children and Families Act 2014 is still the law.** Local authorities are still legally responsible for securing the provision written into an EHCP. Parents still have the right to appeal decisions and to challenge failures. In February 2026, **Education Minister Georgia Gould publicly confirmed that children with special needs will keep their current support and placements**.

**No child has lost their legal protections because of reform discussions.** No existing EHCP has been removed or downgraded because of proposed changes.

#### Rights still in force as of 15 February 2026

- ✅ Children and Families Act 2014 remains the legal framework
- ✅ EHCPs remain statutory and legally enforceable
- ✅ Right to request an EHCP assessment at any time
- ✅ Right to appeal to the SEND Tribunal
- ✅ Right to choose a school and have it named in the EHCP
- ✅ Local authorities remain legally responsible for EHCP provision
- ✅ SEND Code of Practice remains in force
- ✅ 20-week statutory timescale unchanged

#### The law has not changed

The most important point for parents to understand is this. **The law has not moved.** The Children and Families Act 2014 still governs SEND in England. **Any major change to EHCP eligibility, appeal rights, or legal duties would require new primary legislation.** That has not happened.

#### EHCPs are still enforceable

**An EHCP is still a legal document.** The provision written into it must be delivered. Section F remains enforceable. If provision is missing, delayed, or reduced without agreement, **families still have the right to challenge**.

#### Appeal rights remain

**Parents still have the right to appeal:** refusals to assess, refusals to issue an EHCP, the content of an EHCP, and the named placement.

#### No child is being moved or reclassified

**There is no confirmed policy that removes EHCPs from children already holding them.** There is no confirmed policy that forces children out of specialist provision or into mainstream.

## What We Know So Far

### What we know so far: the current state of SEND in England

**Status: Confirmed** | Last updated: 15th February 2026

#### 60-Second Summary

The evidence shows the SEND system is overwhelmed. Many more children need support than a decade ago, but **support quality and outcomes have not improved**. Families wait too long, councils overspend, schools struggle, and too many parents are pushed into legal action just to get help. Research across government, councils, and Parliament agrees **this is a system problem, not a parenting problem**. The growth in plans and appeals reflects unmet need earlier on.

#### Key Statistics

- **576,000+** children with EHCPs (140% increase since 2015)
- **1.2m** pupils on SEN Support (without an EHCP)
- **£9.4bn** high needs funding (2024/25)
- **~50%** EHCPs issued on time (approx. within 20 weeks)
- **90%** parent tribunal wins (of decided cases)

#### Why demand keeps rising

EHCP numbers have increased sharply since 2014. **This is not because parents are gaming the system.** It reflects better identification, rising complexity, and the fact that an EHCP is often the only route to enforceable, funded support.

#### The funding picture

Council spending on high needs rose from **£7.8 billion in 2015 to £13.1 billion in 2024**. Despite this, 95% of local authorities are running SEND budget deficits. Local authorities have accumulated **over £3 billion of SEND funding deficits**.

#### What this means for parents

**Nearly 50% of parents describe securing SEND support as stressful.** 22% say their child's school is not delivering the support written into the EHCP. **The rights and routes available to you today still work.** Use them.

## What Is Being Discussed

### Under discussion: what is being debated publicly right now

**Status: Discussed** | Last updated: 15th February 2026

#### 60-Second Summary

Alongside confirmed changes, there is active public discussion about deeper reform of SEND in England. **Nothing in this section is law. Nothing here has been enacted.** These are ideas, proposals, and areas of debate that may shape future policy but are not settled.

The common thread is this. The government is trying to reduce delay, conflict, and unsustainable cost, while families and professionals are trying to protect children's rights and access to real support. **The tension sits between system efficiency and individual safeguards.**

#### Key Statistics

- **100+** DfE engagement events (consultation work)
- **130,000+** petition signatures (Save Our Children's Rights)
- **90%** parent tribunal win rate (prompting system design debate)
- A SEND focused **Schools White Paper is expected in 2026**.
- A national SEND reform consultation ran from December 2025 to January 2026.
- Parliamentary committees are **actively scrutinising reform proposals**.

#### Tiered models of support

One of the most discussed ideas is whether **SEND support should be more clearly tiered**:

- **Tier 4 (EHCP):** Full legal plan for children with the most severe and complex needs. Enforceable provision.
- **Tier 3 (Specialist):** Specialist involvement without a full EHCP. Key question: would this be legally enforceable?
- **Tier 2 (Targeted):** Small group interventions and additional support, coordinated by the school.
- **Tier 1 (Universal):** Teacher-led accommodations available to all pupils as standard classroom practice.

**No tiered model has been confirmed.** This reflects ideas under discussion, not policy.

#### Reducing over-reliance on EHCPs

There is broad agreement that **too many families feel forced to seek EHCPs just to secure basic support**. The discussion is about how to strengthen support earlier, not whether EHCPs should exist.

#### Funding models and incentives

One idea being discussed is **giving groups of schools pooled budgets to commission services like speech therapy or educational psychology collectively**. Parents are rightly asking **how accountability would work if funding is less individualised**.

#### Appeals and adversarial processes

One proposal under discussion is **making mediation a compulsory step before tribunal appeal**. **There is no confirmed proposal to remove appeal rights.**

#### Mainstream inclusion and specialist provision

The discussion is about balance, not absolutes. **No policy currently forces children into mainstream against their needs.**

## What The Leaks Do Not Mean

### What leaks do not mean, and why they happen at all

**Status: Confirmed** | Last updated: 15th February 2026

#### 60-Second Summary

Leaks are not designed to help parents. **They are designed to test ideas, manage risk, and shape reaction.** They arrive without safeguards, without timelines, and without explanation.

**A leak does not mean a decision has been made.** It does not mean your child is about to lose support. It does not change the law.

#### Until the law changes, these facts hold

- ✅ EHCPs remain statutory and enforceable
- ✅ Local authorities remain responsible for securing provision in EHCPs
- ✅ Parents retain the right to request assessment
- ✅ Parents retain the right to appeal decisions

**A leak cannot switch these off. A headline does not override legislation.**

#### Why governments leak or kite fly ideas

Common reasons: testing reaction without commitment, softening the ground for later proposals, surfacing problems in early thinking, managing disagreement inside government, shifting the narrative towards change being inevitable.

#### What leaks usually leave out

**Leaks strip away the parts that matter most to families:** Who would be protected. What happens to children who already have EHCPs. How transitions would work. What funding would sit behind any new model. What challenge or appeal routes would exist.

**Without these details, any idea will sound harsher and more threatening than it would be in practice.**

#### The fear underneath the headline

Most parents are not afraid of reform in theory. **They are afraid of loss.** These fears are rational. They come from lived experience, not overreaction.

#### Acting versus reacting

**Acting because your child needs protection is grounded.** Acting only because of a headline often leads to burnout. Fear-driven urgency drains energy that children need from you.

#### The bottom line, without false reassurance

**You are allowed to feel scared and still think clearly.**

Right now:
- Reform is being discussed.
- Some improvements are confirmed and underway.
- **Major rights changes are not confirmed.**
- **The law protecting your child has not changed.**

**You are not missing a secret deadline. You are not already too late.**

## What This Could Mean

### What this could mean

Practical implications of confirmed and proposed changes for different groups.

#### Key Points

- This section translates policy information into possible practical implications.
- We focus on what changes could mean, not predictions about what will happen.
- Implications vary significantly depending on individual circumstances.
- This is not advice. Seek specialist support for individual situations.

#### Current system vs proposed direction

| Current System | Proposed Direction |
|---|---|

## EHCPs: Complete Guide

### EHCPs: a complete guide for parents

**Status: Confirmed** | Last updated: 15th February 2026

#### 60-Second Summary

An Education, Health and Care Plan (EHCP) is a **legally binding document** for children and young people aged 0 to 25 with significant special educational needs. It coordinates support across education, health, and social care. Since 2014, the number of children with EHCPs has **more than doubled to over 576,000**. The process should take 20 weeks but only about half are issued on time. Parents have strong legal rights at every stage, including the right to appeal, and **win 99% of tribunal cases that reach a hearing**.

#### What is an EHCP

An EHCP is a statutory document introduced by the **Children and Families Act 2014**, replacing the older Statement of SEN system. It is the only legal mechanism in England that guarantees coordinated, funded support across education, health, and social care. The education provisions in an EHCP are **legally enforceable**.

#### Who qualifies

A child or young person may qualify for an EHCP if they have **special educational needs that cannot be met through the support ordinarily available at their school**. There is no legal requirement to exhaust school support first — if needs are severe, assessment should not be delayed.

Need areas: Communication and interaction, Cognition and learning, Social emotional and mental health, Sensory and physical.

#### EHCP statistics

- **576,000+** children with EHCPs (January 2024)
- **138,242** assessment requests (in 2023 alone)
- **140%** growth since 2015
- **50%** issued on time (within 20 weeks)
- **95%** assessments lead to plan (once LA agrees to assess)
- **99%** parents win at tribunal (of decided cases)
- **24%** requests refused (~33,000 children turned away in 2023)
- **25,000** tribunal appeals (registered in 2024/25)
- **£153m** LAs spent on appeals (in 2023-24 alone)
- **1.3%** LA win rate at tribunal (of cases that go to hearing)

#### What an EHCP contains (Sections A-K)

- **A** — Views and aspirations
- **B** — Special educational needs (every need must have matching provision in Section F)
- **C** — Health needs related to SEN
- **D** — Social care needs (often left blank)
- **E** — Outcomes (goals for next 2-3 years)
- **F** — Special educational provision (most important, legally binding)
- **G** — Health provision (NHS services)
- **H1/H2** — Social care provision
- **I** — School placement
- **J** — Personal budget (optional)
- **K** — Appendices (all reports and evidence)

#### Your rights

Parents have the right to: request assessment at any time, express a preference for school, appeal decisions to the SEND Tribunal, request mediation, request a personal budget, attend and contribute to annual reviews.

#### Common challenges

Vague wording in Section F, delayed assessments, incomplete Sections C and D, and placement disputes.

#### Tribunals and appeals

The tribunal is free to use, parents can represent themselves, and the **parent success rate is 99% at hearing**. LA win rate is just 1.3%.
