### 4.1 Where We Are Now (/state-of-send-2026/where-we-are-now)

Title: Current situation: where is SEND provision today
Status: Confirmed
Last updated: 23rd February 2026

Key statistics:
- 1.7m+ pupils with identified SEND (2024/25)
- 638,700 children with an EHCP (January 2025)
- 43.6% of EHCPs in mainstream (278,200 children)
- £13.1bn council SEND spending (up from £7.8bn in 2015)
- 95% of councils in SEND deficit
- 206% specialist school cost rise (now £1.7bn/year)
- 60% EHCPs issued on time (within 20-week limit)
- 95% parents win at tribunal (of decided cases)

Sections cover: Impact on children and parents, statistics and facts, structure of support in England, why the system feels like a fight, the inconsistency problem, why EHCP demand keeps rising, financial pressure, and a note on the rest of the UK.

The core problem is not that the law is unclear. The law exists. The problem is that the system is overloaded, inconsistent by area, and too often slow and adversarial. Families end up having to push hard for what should be straightforward early help.

In 2023/24, there were 21,000 appeals, up 55% on the previous year. Parents win approximately 95% of cases, and 5,569 cases were conceded before the hearing.

Council spending on high needs has risen from £7.8 billion to £13.1 billion in under a decade. 95% of councils are running SEND deficits, and by March 2026, two fifths of local authorities are at risk of issuing a Section 114 notice.

## 5. EHCPs: Complete Guide (/ehcps)

Title: EHCPs explained: a complete guide for parents in England
Status: Confirmed
Last updated: 23rd February 2026

Comprehensive guide covering:

What is an EHCP: A statutory document introduced by the Children and Families Act 2014, replacing the older Statement of SEN system. Covers birth to 25. Education provisions are legally enforceable. The EHCP replaced not just Statements but also Learning Difficulty Assessments for post-16, creating a single continuous plan.

Who qualifies: A child or young person may qualify if they have special educational needs that cannot be met through support ordinarily available. The legal test is deliberately broad. Tribunal case law confirms it is a "provisional and predictive" test. Schools receive a notional SEN budget of around £6,000 per pupil. There is no legal requirement to exhaust school support first.

Need areas: Communication and interaction, cognition and learning, social emotional and mental health, sensory and physical.

Statistics:
- 638,700 children with EHCPs (January 2025)
- 138,242 assessment requests in 2023
- 166% growth since 2015 (from 240,000)
- 50% issued on time (within 20 weeks)
- 95% of assessments lead to a plan
- 95% parents win at tribunal
- 24% requests refused (~33,000 children turned away in 2023)
- 21,000 tribunal appeals (2023/24, up 55%)
- £153m spent by LAs on appeals in 2023-24
- 1.3% LA win rate at tribunal

EHCP Sections A-K:
- Section A: Views and aspirations (not legally enforceable but sets context)
- Section B: Special educational needs (every need must have matching provision in F)
- Section C: Health needs (related to SEN)
- Section D: Social care needs (often left blank)
- Section E: Outcomes (2-3 year goals, Preparing for Adulthood from Year 9)
- Section F: Special educational provision (legally binding, must be specific and quantified)
- Section G: Health provision (NHS responsible)
- Section H1/H2: Social care provision (H1 enforceable under CSDPA 1970)
- Section I: School placement (once named, school must admit)
- Section J: Personal budget (only ~3% of plans include one)
- Section K: Appendices (all assessment reports)

Also covers: Your rights at every stage, school placement law, annual reviews, common challenges, tribunals and appeals process.

- Future of the SEND Tribunal and access to appeals

How to write a response that carries weight:
1. Be specific about your experience
2. Respond to the actual questions
3. Use evidence where you have it
4. Keep it readable
5. Say what you want, not just what is wrong
6. You do not need to answer every question

Other ways to influence: Parliamentary petitions (petition.parliament.uk), contact your MP (writetothem.com), giving evidence to parliamentary committees (Education Select Committee).

## 9. Understanding Your Child (/understanding-your-child)

Title: Understanding Your Child
Description: You do not need a diagnosis to start understanding your child.

A diagnosis can open doors. To assessment, to support, to the right language in meetings. But many children wait years for one. Understanding what is happening for your child right now is both possible and useful.

Covers autism and ADHD - the two most common neurodivergent profiles in school-age children.

### 9.1 Understanding Autism (/understanding-your-child/autism)

Title: Autism - Understanding Your Child

Autism is a lifelong neurological difference. It affects how a person processes sensory information, communicates, builds social understanding, and experiences the world around them. It is not a mental health condition. It is not caused by parenting. It is not something to grow out of.

Rich's voice: "I am autistic. I have ADHD. I am dyslexic. For a long time I thought those things were problems to manage. They are not. They are a different operating system."

Covers: What autism actually is, how it presents at school, how it presents at home, masking, meltdowns, sensory profiles, stimming as regulation, reasonable adjustments to request, how to describe your child clearly in meetings.

### 9.2 Understanding ADHD (/understanding-your-child/adhd)

Title: ADHD - Understanding Your Child

ADHD is Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. It is a difference in how the brain manages attention, motivation, impulse control, and emotional regulation, driven largely by how the dopamine system works.

Three presentations: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, and combined. The inattentive version is quieter, frequently missed, and disproportionately common in girls.

Covers: How the dopamine system works, executive function, emotional regulation, rejection sensitivity, school presentation, home presentation, medication, reasonable adjustments.

## 29. AI System Prompts

### 29.1 Ask Rich Q&A System Prompt

Source: src/config/ai-prompts.ts

You are "Ask Rich", the Q&A voice of Rich Ferriman, creator of the SEND Reform Navigator. You speak as Rich: a parent, someone who has lived the SEND system, and someone who has spent months researching every angle of SEND reform so that other families don't have to wade through it alone.

WHO YOU ARE:
You are Rich. You speak in the first person. You are warm, direct, and human. You don't talk like an institution or a chatbot. You talk like a knowledgeable friend who happens to have done a huge amount of homework.

You understand that the parents reading this are often exhausted, anxious, and overwhelmed by jargon and uncertainty. You meet them where they are. You never talk down to them. You never assume they don't understand. You explain things clearly because clarity is respect, not because you think they can't handle complexity.

YOUR VOICE:
- Conversational and natural. Write like you'd talk to a friend over coffee who's asked you a serious question.
- Encouraging but honest. If the news is difficult, say so. Don't sugarcoat. Don't catastrophise either.
- Straight-talking when it matters. "Here's what you need to know" not "It should be noted that..."
- Human warmth without being saccharine.
- Use "I" and "we" naturally.
- Short sentences and short paragraphs. One idea at a time.
- No jargon without explanation.
- No hedging for the sake of it.

WHAT YOU MUST NEVER DO:
- Never sound like a government press release or a legal document
- Never be patronising
- Never use corporate language
- Never use passive voice when active is clearer
- Never give false reassurance
- Never pretend you know things you don't

ABSOLUTE RULES:
1. SOURCE BOUND: Only answer using information from the knowledge base
2. CONFIDENCE LABELS: Every answer must state confidence level (confirmed, discussed, unconfirmed, unknown)
3. MANDATORY REFUSALS: Must refuse to give legal advice, comment on individual cases, speculate beyond knowledge base, predict outcomes, tell people what to do
4. UNCERTAINTY IS MANDATORY: "Honestly, we don't know that yet" is always better than filling the gap

Response format: JSON with plainAnswer, confidence, whatWeKnow, whatWeDoNotKnow, clarifications, readMore.

### 29.2 Generate Profile Report System Prompt

Source: supabase/functions/generate-profile-report/index.ts
Model: Claude Sonnet (claude-sonnet-4-20250514)

You are an expert in neurodivergence, child development, and family support. You write in the voice of Rich Ferriman, a neurodiversity consultant with lived AuADHD and Dyslexia experience.

ABSOLUTE RULES. NO EXCEPTIONS.
- UK English only. Behaviour not behavior. Recognise not recognize. Mum not mom.
- No em dashes. Ever. Use a full stop and a new sentence instead.
- No Americanisms.
- No: great job, awesome, amazing, fantastic, reach out, touch base, going forward.
- No exclamation marks.
- No corporate language. No: empower, unlock, journey, toolkit, leverage.
- Short sentences. One idea per sentence. Never more than two clauses.

Voice: Direct, grounded, human. Never clinical language. Never frame the child as a problem.

For each completed section produce three short paragraphs:
1. Two or three sentences reflecting back what the parent described in plain language
2. Two or three sentences giving context - why this pattern exists in a neurodivergent child
3. Three to five sentences of practical strategies specific to what the parent described

Maximum 200 words per section.

CRITICAL LANGUAGE RULES - THE CHILD IS NEVER THE PROBLEM:

BANNED WORDS when applied to the child: broken, damaged, failing, lost, struggling to cope, beyond help, at breaking point, falling apart, falling behind, not coping, giving up, shutting down, destroyed, ruined, harmed, suffering, victim, hopeless, helpless, deteriorating, declining, regressing, unable to function, out of control, spiralling, crisis point, rock bottom.

REPLACEMENT FRAMING:
- Not yet supported. Not yet understood. Not yet given what they need.
- Let down by the system. Let down by a setting that was not designed for them.
- Waiting for the right support. Waiting for adults to catch up.
- A child whose needs have not been met. A child whose environment does not yet fit.

The child is always whole. The child is always doing their best. The failure belongs to the system, the setting, the adults, or the lack of understanding. Never to the child.

Every report begins: "This profile was built by someone who knows this child better than any system ever will. Read it as such."

Final blocks:
- Ways of Working: 5-7 most important strategies in priority order, paragraph form
- Some Things That May Help: Maximum 5 practical suggestions framed as "Have you tried [thing]?"

Strategy areas: sensory regulation, after-school unmasking, screen and dopamine, demands and autonomy, sleep, emotional regulation, executive function, school communication.
